How to Replace Tires

Make sure you're wearing gloves and safety glasses to ensure proper protection from harmful chemicals.

How Often Should You Replace Your Tires?

  1. The best way to know if it's time to buy new tires for your vehicle is to have them inspected by a professional, but there are ways to check them yourself. If you want a precise service interval, check your owner's manual or find yours here.
  2. You can also use the Penny Test to check your tire tread. This test can be performed in your own driveway in just a few minutes. Here's how it works:
    • Place a penny in the tread of your tire.
    • Turn the penny so that Lincoln's head points down into the tread.
    • See if the top of Lincoln's head “disappears.” If it does, your tread is still above 2/32” and you're good to go. If you can see his entire head, it may be time to replace the tire because your tread is no longer deep enough. (see Fig. 1 for example)
    • Check all four tires, especially in areas that look the most worn. If any parts of your tire fail the penny test, it may be time to replace the tire.

Tools to Change a Tire:

  1. A manual car jack designed to raise your vehicle high enough to remove the flat tire.
  2. A spare tire. Most vehicles are equipped with a “donut tire”, a spare that is smaller in diameter than a regular tire.
  3. A lug wrench or torque wrench. This wrench is designed to remove the nuts attaching the wheel to the rotor.
  4. Wheel wedges. Also known as wheel chocks are placed against the wheels to immobilize the vehicle.
  5. A portable tire inflator.
  6. A tire gauge to read the recommended tire pressure.

Steps:

  1. If your tire has gone flat on the road, follow the following key steps:
    • First, look for a safe place to change the tire. Pull off to a quiet side street or a store parking lot and park on a flat section of pavement. Keep alert and aware of your surroundings when exiting your vehicle and during the entire process.
    • If you are on a busy road or highway when the tire blows out, slowly reduce your speed and look for a safe spot to pull over away from traffic, such as the shoulder of an off-ramp.
    • If there is no exit nearby, locate a long straight section of the highway and pull over in the emergency lane or on the shoulder of the road, as far away from traffic as you can.
  2. Make Yourself Seen, In Case Of an Emergency
    • Turn on your hazard lights as you bring your vehicle to a stop. If it is evening or late night, try to find a well-lit area and turn on the dome light inside your car.
    • Put the car in park and engage the parking brake to keep the car from rolling. If you have wheel chocks or wheel wedges in your tools to change a tire, place them firmly against both wheels opposite the flat one. If you do not have wheel chocks or wedges in your tool kit, use a couple of bricks or large stones to keep the car immobile.
  3. Gather the tools mentioned earlier
    • Set out the tools to change a tire in a place where you can easily and safely get to it. The car jack and lug wrench should be the closest within reach.
  4. Loosen the Lug Nuts.
    • One of the key steps to change a tire properly is to prepare the flat for removal while the car is still on the road. This keeps the tire stable and makes it easier to remove.
    • Remove the wheel cover if your vehicle has one.
    • Use the lug wrench or torque wrench to turn the lug nuts counterclockwise. (see Fig. 2 for example)
    • Loosen the lug nuts but do not remove them.
  5. Raise the Car With the Jack
    • Consult your owner's manual in the section on tools to change a tire for the proper placement of the jack and how to operate it.
    • Place the jack under the vehicle frame next to the flat tire.
    • Raise the car with the jack until the flat tire has a 6-inch clearance from the ground. Do not place any part of your body under the car while you are changing the tire. (see Fig. 3 for example)
  6. Remove the Lug Nuts and the Old Tire
    • Remove the lug nuts and place them together in a safe place. The floor of the front seat or inside the door pocket is a good place to keep them while you work.
    • Take hold of the flat tire with both hands and pull it towards you until it slides off the hub. Set the flat tire aside.
  7. Attach the Spare Tire and Replace the Lug Nuts
    • Line up the spare tire with wheel bolts and push it into place.
    • Replace each lug nut, turning them clockwise to tighten. Tighten the nuts in a star pattern — move to the next nut across, rather than the one adjacent — to be sure the wheel mounts evenly. (see Fig. 4 for example)
    • Turn the lug wrench one final time to be sure the tire is secure, but do not completely tighten all the bolts while the tire is in the air.
  8. Lower the Vehicle and Tighten the Lug Nuts
    • Lower the car so that the spare tire is resting on the ground; the full weight of the car should not yet be on it.
    • Use the lug wrench to tighten the nuts, following the same star pattern as before. Push down on the wrench with your full body weight to be sure the lug nuts are very tight. (see Fig. 4 for example)
    • Replace the wheel cover. Lower the jack and remove it.
  9. Put Tools Back and Check Tire Pressure
    • Check the tire pressure with a tire gauge as one of the final steps to change a tire. If you have a portable tire inflator. as one of your tools to change a tire, use it to add air if needed. If you do not have one, slowly drive to the nearest gas station.
  10. Get the Flat Repaired
    • Once the spare is securely on your car, drive immediately to the nearest repair shop.
    • Most spare tires in cars today are not full-sized and should not be driven on for long distances or at speeds exceeding 50 mph. Get your tire repaired or replaced as soon as possible. Consult your vehicle manual for specific limits regarding your spare tire.
  11. When to Call Roadside Assistance
    • Personal safety: Examine your environment before getting out of the car. If the area you are in doesn't appear to be safe, or you are unable to safely leave the car without walking into traffic, don't put yourself at unnecessary risk to change the tire.
    • Weather hazards: Harsh weather makes the job more difficult. Pouring rain, cascading snow or even blazing heat might make conditions unsuitable. If your safety or health is at risk, consider calling a tow truck.
    • Road conditions: Consider the road conditions before you pull over. If traffic is heavy or fast, if the road is narrow, or if you're unsure of the road, a professional service might be able to handle the work more easily.
